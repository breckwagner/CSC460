* Laser Tank Game

The final project theme for this term is to ultimately create two groups of laser-firing tanks, each of which is implemented by a remote-controlled semi-autonomous Roomba robot. Essentially, it is similar to this final project (and report), designed and implemented by Michael Krazanowski during the summer of 2010.

Our tank game is played by 4 Roomba robots; each is designed and implemented by a team of two students. Two teams play against two teams. Each Roomba robot is semi-autonomous, i.e., it can roam and seek target on its own and at same time avoid any obstacle in its path. A human operator is also allowed to remotely-control the motion of the robot and trigger its laser.

To keep the game easy to compete, each Roomba must roam for 30 seconds and then must remain stationary for 30 seconds. The Roomba is allowed to spin, no faster than 6 degree per second, thus maximum 180 degree in 30 seconds. While roaming, there are no rotation or speed limit.

On top of each Roomba, there is also a light-sensing target. When it is being hit, the Roomba loses its motion capability, thus remains stationary until the game is over.

Within a 10 minute period, the group with the most mobile Roombas wins!

* Requirements

Each team consists of two students, who are responsible to assemble, develop, design, test, measure, evaluate, document both software, hardware, and their interfacing, in this final project. Every team must use an RTOS (developed in project 2) in their implemented code.

There are two phases in this project:
  1. In phase 1, every team must demonstrate a working remotely-controlled Roomba with the ability to roam and shoot a target.
  2. In phase 2, semi-autonomous behaviours must be added to complete the final design, thus the Roomba is able to roam and avoid obstacles on its own.
